Evaluation of Unit 11 against Unit 6

The Unit 6 proposal set out a clear intention to analyse Airbnb pricing in New York City using machine learning techniques. At proposal stage, the emphasis was on defining the business problem, selecting a dataset and identifying a plausible analytical approach. By Unit 11, the project had evolved into a more mature piece of work with clearer feature engineering, stronger justification of methods and a more balanced interpretation of results.

Area Unit 6 proposal Unit 11 final project What changed
Problem framing Focused on pricing optimisation as the main question. Kept pricing optimisation as the core question but added stronger market segmentation logic. The final version addressed both prediction and business segmentation rather than prediction alone.
Analytical approach Proposed regression and clustering as candidate techniques. Implemented multivariate linear regression, K-Means and a comparison with DBSCAN. The final version was more methodical and allowed comparison between clustering approaches.
Data preparation Recognised that cleaning and preprocessing would be necessary. Included explicit handling of zero-price errors, missing values, capped outliers and log transformation of price. The final work showed a more mature understanding of how data preparation affects modelling quality. fileciteturn1file3
Interpretation Expected that models would reveal useful pricing patterns. Showed that borough and room type were dominant predictors, while remaining variance pointed to missing contextual factors. The final version was more critical about limitations and avoided overstating predictive power. fileciteturn1file2
Business relevance Framed the project in terms of improving pricing decisions. Produced more actionable outputs, including four listing segments with different pricing and demand characteristics. The final project moved closer to a decision-support tool rather than a purely academic model.
Collaboration Work was still being scoped and roles were less defined. Team contributions were more clearly reflected in the completed analysis and written output. The project matured not only technically but also organisationally.

Overall judgement

The comparison shows a clear movement from a sound proposal to a more critically developed final project. The main improvement was not simply that more analysis was completed. The stronger difference was that the final work demonstrated better judgement: it used data preparation more carefully, interpreted results with greater caution and linked outputs more clearly to business decisions. That shift is significant because it reflects a more realistic understanding of applied machine learning.
